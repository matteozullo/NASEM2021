F1 micro vs F1 macro¶

F1 score¶

F1 scores are given by:

$$ F1_i = 2 \frac{Precision_i \times Recall_i}{Precision_i + Recall_i} $$

where $Precision_i = TP_i/(TP_i + FP_i)$, $Recall_i = TP_i/(TP_i+FP_i)$, and $i$ indexes a class.

F1 micro equals accuracy! For multi-class, F1 micro provides the same information provided by the accuracy. That is because F1 micro considers all classes at once, resulting in $ Recall = Precision $. Hence:


$$ F1 \, micro = 2 \frac{Precision \times Recall}{Precision + Recall} = 2 \frac{Precision^2}{Precision} = Precision$$

Furthermore:

$$ F1 \, micro = Precision = TP/(TP+FP) = Accuracy! \; Q.E.D.$$

Aggregation choice: F1 macro vs F1 weighted¶

Really, the aggregation choice is between F1 macro and F1 weighted, where F1 macro is an unweighted aggregation of F1 scores. More formally:

$$ F1 = \sum_i{w_i \frac{TP}{TP + FP}} $$

When $w_i = 1$, we obtain an F1 macro and when $w_i = n_i$ we obtain an F1 weighted ($n_i$ is the sample proportion by label).

Output¶

We can see that F1 micro is effectively equal to Accuracy in the multi-class case (see example below). Also, the aggregate metrics F1 macro and F1 weighted are different from accuracy. Therefore:

F1 macro is the conservative choice as it does not assign a larger weight to the most frequently occurring label.

Additional Metrics¶

We might consider two additional metrics: Cohen's K and Matthews Correlation Coefficient. However, the second metric is really a correlation coefficient between predicted and grand truth cases, hence it is not suitable for multi-class classifiers.

Cohen's K is a clever metric that considers trues occurring by chance alone:

$$Cohen's K = \frac{Agree - AgreeByChance}{1-AgreeByChance} $$

For an exhaustive explanation of what Cohen's K is and the notion of agreement by chance, please refer to this post.
